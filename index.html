// PROJECT 02

Electromechanical Trash Can

Overview

This project focused on designing a linkage mechanism that moves a trash-deposit tray from its loading position to a rear dumping position while keeping the entire motion within the container's boundaries. Trash is first placed on the top tray, and the mechanism then retracts the tray and directs it toward an opening at the back of the container.

One major design requirement was that all pivot points had to be attached to the container's side walls, while also ensuring the tray followed a precise path between its two end positions. Using geometric constraints and linkage analysis, suitable pivot locations and link dimensions were determined to provide smooth and dependable motion. The completed design satisfies all functional requirements and keeps the tray fully enclosed during operation.

Mechanism Overview
Linkage Design

Theory

By applying vector loop equations and geometric constraints, a four-bar linkage was determined to be the simplest mechanism capable of achieving the required motion while remaining completely enclosed within the container. Mathematical models and plotted solutions were used to assess different link length ratios and pivot locations.

Through iterative analysis, an optimal configuration was selected that delivers the necessary angular rotation and translational movement of the tray while ensuring all links remain anchored to the container's side walls. This analytical method provides a generalized framework for designing motion-controlled tray mechanisms in confined spaces, serving as a predictive approach for defining linkage geometry under strict spatial constraints.

Vector Loop Analysis
Plotted Solutions

Motion Study

The project successfully produced a functional linkage mechanism that moves the tray from its loading position to the rear opening while fully complying with the container's spatial constraints. By utilizing kinematic equations, vector loop analysis, and plotted motion trajectories, the tray's position and orientation were accurately controlled throughout its motion.

The defined initial and final positions established the limits of movement, and the final linkage design was validated by tracing the tray's path. Overall, this project demonstrates how mathematical modeling and mechanism theory can be effectively applied to solve practical engineering problems in compact mechanical systems.

// PROJECT 03

Electromechanical Prototype Car

Abstract

This project applied principles of motor speed control and multibody dynamics to develop a system capable of transporting a 1-foot vertical bar between two points separated by up to 10 feet without allowing the bar to tip over. Individually, I used SolidWorks simulations to evaluate multiple motion concepts, analyze stability, and determine an optimal timing strategy.

As part of a team, we built a fully functional prototype using a 12V gear motor, an Arduino Uno, and custom 3D-printed and foam-board components. Through iterative testing, both the mechanical design and control algorithm were refined to maximize speed while maintaining stability. The project concluded with a live in-class competition where each team's prototype demonstrated the motion task. This experience strengthened my skills in simulation, control systems, rapid prototyping, and collaborative engineering design.

Prototype Overview

Theory — Calculations & SolidWorks

For this project, I developed a complete SolidWorks motion simulation of the cart system to validate the calculations underlying the design. The cart was fully modeled with appropriate forces and constraints, and dynamic simulations were conducted to evaluate acceleration, stability, and overall motion behavior.

By comparing simulation results with theoretical predictions, I confirmed that the system performed as intended and identified minor adjustments to improve performance. This process demonstrated the value of virtual testing in verifying design decisions prior to physical prototyping and further strengthened my skills in motion analysis and engineering problem-solving.

Theoretical Calculations

Results — Data Analysis

The motion study confirmed the design's accuracy, validated the calculations, and gave valuable insight into the cart's behavior before building a prototype.

I generated a reaction force-vs.-time plot at the cart's support to show how loads changed during acceleration, steady motion, and deceleration. Comparing the measured profile to theoretical predictions confirmed the reactions stayed within acceptable limits and the system remained mechanically stable.

Reaction Force Plot
Data Analysis Charts

Assembly of Cart

In our design, the side walls also functioned as axial supports for the rods, removing the need for separate mounting components on the cart. To improve stability, we intentionally lowered the center of mass by positioning the base just a few millimeters above the ground based on initial intuition.

During testing, we observed traction issues, as the 3D-printed wheels tended to slip rather than maintain grip on the surface. To resolve this, we added a rubber lining around each wheel's circumference. This adjustment eliminated skidding, reduced wheel wobble, and improved overall traction. The slight increase in diameter at the wheel center also helped maintain consistent ground contact despite minor misalignment. As a result, the cart was able to travel in a straight, stable path without drifting.

Arduino Control System

This Arduino program controls a DC motor to move back and forth over a defined travel distance using a time-based motion profile for smooth speed regulation. The user specifies the travel distance, target maximum speed, and acceleration, and the code determines whether the motion profile is trapezoidal (accelerate → cruise → decelerate) or triangular (accelerate → decelerate when the distance is too short to reach peak speed).

The program then converts the desired speed into a PWM signal using a calibration factor and ramps the PWM from a minimum threshold (to overcome static friction) up to a calculated peak value. If required, it maintains the peak speed during a cruise phase before symmetrically ramping down to a stop. The sequence is executed first in the forward direction, followed by a pause, and then repeated in reverse before completing the cycle. An encoder interrupt continuously counts pulses in the background, with an optional speed calculation function available to support performance monitoring and feedback verification.
